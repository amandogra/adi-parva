Chapter I

The Origin of the Epic

◀ Prev

When both Chitrangada and Vichitravirya died without heirs, Bhishma stood bound by his oath, unable to take the throne or beget sons to continue the line.

The widowed queens of Vichitravirya, Ambika and Ambalika, remained in the palace, their fates tied to a kingdom without an obvious successor.

At this turning, the queen-mother Satyavati called upon her son Vyasa, asking him to raise up children in the royal line through the ancient practice of niyoga, in which a wise man fathers heirs on behalf of the dead.

From these tense and uneasy unions were born three princes: Dhritarashtra, blind from birth; Pandu, pale and gifted in arms; and Vidura, whose wisdom would become the conscience of the court.

Though Pandu would later be crowned king, a curse laid upon him in the forest forbade him ever again to approach his wives in desire.
This curse set the stage for the unusual births of the Pandavas.

With the king barred from ordinary fatherhood, his queen Kunti remembered a mantra once given to her, by which she could invite deities to grant sons marked by their qualities.

Calling first on the god of righteousness, she received Yudhishthira; then the lord of storms, bringing forth Bhima; then the radiant Indra, who granted Arjuna.

Later, her co‑wife Madri invoked the twin Ashvins and bore the younger brothers Nakula and Sahadeva, completing the five who would be known as the Pandavas.
